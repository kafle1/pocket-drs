\begin{center}\textbf{\fontsize{16}{19}\selectfont ABSTRACT}\end{center}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\vspace{0.5cm}

PocketDRS is a low-cost Leg Before Wicket (LBW) decision-support system for cricket that works with a single smartphone camera. Professional systems such as Hawk-Eye need six to eight synchronised high-speed cameras and dedicated hardware, which puts them out of reach of schools, clubs, and training grounds. PocketDRS shows that a careful single-view workflow can still produce practical decision support at those venues. A Flutter app records the delivery and lets the user mark the pitch corners and both sets of stumps. A FastAPI server then detects the ball from motion, colour and a learned detector, links the detections into one smooth path using RANSAC, recovers the camera pose from the marked stumps, and rebuilds the ball trajectory in real-world 3D using projectile-motion physics. A rule-based engine applies the three LBW checks of ICC Rule~36. On a controlled synthetic harness of eight ground-truth deliveries the system recovered the camera position correctly in every scene and returned the correct verdict in all eight cases (8/8); on a real handheld off-spin clip it produced a plausible end-to-end NOT~OUT verdict within honest monocular bounds, and on an unsuitable real clip it safely declined to invent a decision.

\vspace{1cm}

\noindent
\textbf{Keywords:} Cricket, Decision Review System, Monocular 3D Reconstruction, Computer Vision, LBW.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Introduction}

Modern cricket relies heavily on technology to review close decisions, and Leg Before Wicket (LBW) is one of the hardest of those decisions for the human eye. At the top level the game uses Hawk-Eye, which reconstructs the ball trajectory from six to eight synchronised high-speed cameras and dedicated processing hardware. Hawk-Eye is accurate, but the equipment, installation, and operating cost place it well beyond the reach of schools, clubs, local leagues, and coaching grounds, where the same close decisions occur every week with no review support at all.

PocketDRS addresses this gap. It is a decision-support system that runs on a normal smartphone and a lightweight cloud service. The user records or picks a short clip of the delivery, taps the four corners of the visible pitch to calibrate, sends the clip to the server, and gets back a 3D visualisation together with an LBW verdict. The aim is practical decision support at low cost, not broadcast-grade precision; the system is therefore positioned as an aid to umpiring rather than a replacement for it.

The contribution of the project is to show that a carefully constrained single-camera pipeline, built around the known geometry of a cricket pitch and the physics of projectile motion, can produce consistent and human-readable LBW review support end-to-end. The working prototype demonstrates this with a complete mobile-to-cloud system that combines computer vision, geometric reconstruction, and cricket-specific decision logic.

\section{Problem Statement}

Professional DRS systems depend on multiple synchronized cameras to recover depth and reconstruct the ball trajectory in three dimensions. This produces strong results, but the required equipment, installation effort, and operating cost make such systems inaccessible outside major venues.

When the same decision-review problem is shifted to a single consumer camera, several technical and practical difficulties arise:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A single camera cannot directly see depth, so the system has to recover distance indirectly, for example from how big the ball looks in the image or from how its motion follows the laws of physics.
    \item A cricket ball is small, fast, and motion-blurred, and can easily be confused with background objects, players, shadows, or spectators.
    \item Small errors in marking the pitch corners produce much larger errors at the far end of the pitch, because the far end occupies only a few pixels.
    \item Lighting changes, camera shake, the bowler or batter blocking the view, and inconsistent recording conditions all make a real ground less predictable than a controlled broadcast environment.
\end{enumerate}

Therefore, the central problem addressed by this project is whether a single-phone-camera workflow, supported by pitch calibration and lightweight computer-vision techniques, can produce a sufficiently consistent and understandable LBW review aid for ordinary cricket grounds.

\section{Objectives}

\subsection{General Objective}

To design and implement a low-cost cricket decision-support system that uses a single camera view to analyze a delivery, estimate the ball path in calibrated pitch coordinates, and assist LBW review.

\subsection{Specific Objectives}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Study existing decision-review systems and relevant literature on monocular sports tracking.
    \item Develop a user-guided calibration process based on pitch corners and stump reference points.
    \item Detect and track the ball from delivery video using combined motion and colour cues.
    \item Recover the camera pose from the marked stumps and lift tracked pixel positions to real-world 3D using the calibrated geometry.
    \item Detect important events such as bounce and impact from the tracked trajectory using physics-based fitting.
    \item Estimate an LBW outcome using rule-based checks on pitching line, impact line, and projected stump contact.
    \item Present the result through an interactive 3D visualization that helps users interpret the decision.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scope and Limitation}

\subsection{Scope}

The PocketDRS project encompasses the complete workflow from video capture to LBW decision:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Mobile application for video selection, trimming, and pitch calibration.
    \item Backend processing pipeline for ball detection, tracking, and 3D reconstruction.
    \item LBW decision engine implementing ICC Rule 36.
    \item Interactive 3D visualization of the trajectory.
    \item Local and cloud-based persistent storage of pitches and analyses.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The system operates under umpire-POV camera placement; other angles may require retraining or recalibration.
    \item Accuracy degrades with heavy ball occlusion, poor lighting, or unstable camera mounting.
    \item The system is not ICC-certified and is designed for decision support rather than final authority.
    \item Monocular depth estimation remains fundamentally limited compared to multi-camera triangulation.
    \item Performance depends on proper pitch calibration; user error in marking reference points reduces decision reliability.
\end{itemize}

\section{Development Methodology}

PocketDRS followed an incremental, agile approach: requirement analysis (from literature and existing systems), proof-of-concept on calibration / detection / 3D reconstruction, iterative development of the full pipeline with continuous testing, unit and synthetic-harness validation, and deployment of the backend on Google Cloud Run with Firestore persistence alongside the mobile app on Android and iOS.

Chapter~2 presents the background and literature review. Chapter~3 covers system analysis and design (requirements, feasibility, ER, DFD, architecture, and the five-step algorithm). Chapter~4 details implementation and testing, including unit / system tests, synthetic validation, and real-world clips. Chapter~5 concludes with lessons learned and future recommendations. References and appendices follow.

\chapter{Background Study and Literature Review}

\section{Background Study}

\textbf{Pinhole Camera Model and Intrinsics.} A pinhole camera is the simplest model of any camera: light goes through one tiny hole and lands on a flat sensor behind it. A smartphone lens behaves close enough to this model for our purposes. The four numbers that describe the camera itself (focal length $f_x, f_y$ and principal point $c_x, c_y$) are packed into a $3\times3$ matrix called $K$, which converts a 3D world point into a pixel. Zhang's checkerboard method~\cite{zhang2000} is the standard way to measure $K$. PocketDRS skips the checkerboard and estimates $K$ from the frame size and a typical phone horizontal field of view of $67^{\circ}$.

\textbf{Camera Pose (PnP).} The camera pose is simply where the camera is standing and which way it is pointing, written as a rotation $R$ and a translation $t$. The Perspective-n-Point algorithm~\cite{bradski2008} works out $R$ and $t$ from a handful of known 3D points and the pixels they appear at. It finds the pose that makes those known points line up best with the image. PocketDRS feeds it the four tapped pitch corners.

\textbf{Homography.} A homography is a $3\times3$ matrix $H$ that links two different views of the same flat surface~\cite{hartley2004}. Because a cricket pitch is flat, one homography fits the whole pitch. Once $H$ is known from the four corner taps, any pitch pixel can be converted to a real position in metres. RANSAC~\cite{fischler1981} throws away wrong point matches before $H$ is fitted.

\textbf{Projectile Motion.} A thrown ball flies in a predictable curve, set by gravity, because the wind drag in a short delivery is small enough to ignore. Its position obeys
\[
X(t) = X_0 + V_x t,\quad Y(t) = Y_0 + V_y t,\quad Z(t) = Z_0 + V_z t - \tfrac{1}{2} g t^2,
\]
with $g = 9.81$~m/s$^2$. In plain words: the ball moves at a steady speed sideways and forward, and falls under gravity. Any candidate path that does not follow this shape cannot be a real cricket ball. The bounce is treated as the vertical speed flipping sign and shrinking by a fixed factor (coefficient of restitution $\approx 0.55$).

\textbf{RANSAC.} RANSAC is a way to fit a model when most of the data is wrong~\cite{fischler1981}. It picks a tiny random sample, fits a candidate model to it, and counts how many other points agree. After many tries it keeps the model with the most agreement. PocketDRS uses RANSAC twice: once to fit the homography and once to link the per-frame ball detections into one path.

\textbf{Kalman Filter (for reference).} A Kalman filter is the textbook way to track a moving object frame by frame. PocketDRS does not use one. RANSAC plus a single projectile-curve fit handled clutter better and was easier to explain. An Extended Kalman Filter remains the obvious upgrade for live tracking.

\textbf{ICC Rule 36 (LBW).} Law~36 of cricket~\cite{icc} sets the LBW rule. Three things must all be true at once: the ball pitches in line with the stumps, strikes the pad in line, and would have gone on to hit the stumps. A small $\approx 25$~mm margin around the stump edge is left to the on-field umpire (``umpire's call''). PocketDRS checks all three conditions and respects the margin.

\section{Literature Review}

Owens \textit{et al.}~\cite{owens2003} described the original Hawk-Eye ball tracker. It uses six to eight synchronised cameras and custom calibration to rebuild ball paths to a few millimetres. \textbf{Why this matters for PocketDRS:} this is the benchmark PocketDRS is compared against, and the source of the multi-camera cost problem the project tries to side-step.

Zhang~\cite{zhang2000} introduced a flexible camera-calibration method using a printed checkerboard. It removed the need for expensive calibration rigs and is still the textbook way to measure focal length and principal point. \textbf{Why this matters for PocketDRS:} the same intrinsic-parameter model is what $K$ in our pinhole equation refers to, even though we estimate $K$ from FOV instead of running the full checkerboard.

Fischler and Bolles~\cite{fischler1981} proposed RANSAC, a way to fit a model when many of the data points are wrong. It samples small random subsets, fits a candidate, and keeps the one that explains the most points. \textbf{Why this matters for PocketDRS:} we use RANSAC twice, once for the pitch homography and once to link noisy per-frame ball detections into one trajectory.

Hartley and Zisserman~\cite{hartley2004} wrote the standard reference on multiple-view geometry, including homography and projective reconstruction. \textbf{Why this matters for PocketDRS:} the single-view geometry used to map pitch pixels to metres comes straight from this text.

Triggs \textit{et al.}~\cite{triggs2000} formalised bundle adjustment as the joint least-squares refinement of camera parameters and 3D structure. \textbf{Why this matters for PocketDRS:} the projectile-fit step uses the same least-squares idea, only with a physics-based parametric curve instead of a free 3D point cloud.

Ponglertnapakorn and Suwajanakorn~\cite{ponglertnapakorn2025} recovered 3D ball trajectories from a single 2D track by using the physics of ball motion as the constraint. \textbf{Why this matters for PocketDRS:} this is the closest published prior work and is the direct evidence that monocular 3D ball reconstruction is feasible at all.

Bochinski \textit{et al.}~\cite{bochinski2017} showed that high-quality multi-frame tracking is possible by linking detections frame-to-frame using simple bounding-box overlap (IoU). \textbf{Why this matters for PocketDRS:} the same detection-linking idea is used here, with a projectile-motion model in place of plain box overlap to reject background clutter.

\textbf{Synthesis.} Single-view sports analysis becomes possible when two things hold: the scene contains a known geometric reference (the pitch) and the motion follows a strong rule (projectile flight). PocketDRS uses both, and adds the mobile capture, cloud processing, and visualisation needed to make it usable end-to-end.

\subsection{Feasibility Analysis}

\textbf{Technical Feasibility}

The system uses well-known, mature tools. Flutter handles the mobile app on Android and iOS. FastAPI runs the backend. OpenCV, NumPy, and SciPy do the computer-vision work. Firebase handles sign-in and cloud storage. A working prototype already runs the full upload-to-result flow on ordinary phones, so the stack is proven.

\textbf{Operational Feasibility}

The workflow is short and repeatable. The user places a phone behind the bowler-end stumps, records the delivery, taps four pitch corners to calibrate, trims the clip, and waits for the result. No training is needed beyond a short on-screen guide. It fits coaching nets, schools, and clubs.

\textbf{Economic Feasibility}

The cost is essentially the phone and a tripod. There are no camera arrays, no licensed broadcast hardware, and no paid SDKs; the software is open source and the cloud runs on a free-tier-friendly Cloud Run service. A professional DRS install runs into hundreds of thousands of dollars; PocketDRS runs for the price of any modern smartphone.

\textbf{Schedule Feasibility}

The project fits one semester. Requirements and design took weeks 1--3, the pipeline and app took weeks 4--12, and validation and the report took the final three weeks. Each module was built and tested on its own before being wired together, which kept slippage local.

\subsection{Architectural Design}

PocketDRS has four parts: client, API, pipeline, and storage. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows how they connect.

\section{Algorithm Details}

The server analysis runs in five steps, one after the other. Each step takes a small, named input, produces a small, named output, and is described in plain words below. The maths behind each step is standard computer vision and is cited in the references; the goal here is to make the \emph{what} and \emph{why} of each step easy to follow.

\subsection{Step 1: Finding Where the Camera Was Standing}

\textbf{Given:} the pitch corners and the two sets of stumps (base and top) the user marked on the first frame, and the known real-world geometry of a cricket wicket (stump width and height).

\textbf{Produces:} the camera's position and the direction it was pointing.

Because a cricket pitch is always the same size (about 20~m long and 3~m wide), the system can compare the shape of the pitch as it appears in the image with its known real shape. From the difference between the two, it works out where the camera must have been standing and which way it was facing. This is a well-known technique in computer vision. One known problem is that the maths has two possible answers, one with the camera above the ground and one mirrored below it; the system simply rejects the below-ground answer, since a real camera is always above the pitch.

\subsection{Step 2: Finding the Ball in Each Frame}

\textbf{Given:} each video frame, and the user-selected ball colour.

\textbf{Produces:} a list of candidate ball positions in each frame, each with a confidence score.

The ball is found in two independent ways and the results are combined:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{By motion.} The system compares each frame with a model of the still background and keeps only the parts that are moving. It then looks for ball-shaped moving objects. Importantly, it accepts the ball in two forms: as a small \emph{round blob} (a slow or distant ball), and as a short \emph{blurred streak} (a fast ball). A fast cricket ball filmed at 30 frames per second smears into a streak rather than a clean circle, so looking only for circles would miss it.
    \item \textbf{By colour.} The system also looks for regions matching the ball's colour (for example, the red of a cricket ball).
\end{enumerate}

When a motion detection and a colour detection agree on the same spot, the system is more confident that it has found the real ball.

\paragraph{Ignoring fixed clutter.}
Before the next step, the system removes any detection that keeps appearing in the \emph{same} place throughout the clip. A real ball only passes through any given spot once or twice; a red or round object that sits still the whole time (a logo, a helmet, a bag, a bat handle) is not the ball. Any candidate that appears in more than 30\% of the frames at the same location is treated as background and discarded. This is the single most important fix for real-world handheld videos: without it, the next step would happily draw a ``ball path'' through a cluster of fixed clutter.

\subsection{Step 3: Joining the Detections into One Ball Path}

\textbf{Given:} the list of candidate ball positions from Step 2.

\textbf{Produces:} a single smooth path through the frames, plus a count of how many detections fit it and how well.

Step 2 returns the real ball plus some false alarms. Step 3 finds the one consistent path that runs through them. It picks a pair of early detections as a guess, draws the smooth curve a real ball would follow from that pair, and counts how many later detections sit on that curve. The guess that explains the most detections wins.

Two small design choices make this work for phone video shot from behind the stumps. First, a ball flying \emph{towards} the camera barely moves sideways on screen, so the inlier test uses total motion across the whole clip rather than frame-to-frame speed. Second, if the best path the search finds covers only a tiny slice of the image, it is treated as leftover clutter; the system then reports ``no clear ball trajectory'' instead of guessing.

\subsection{Step 4: Rebuilding the Ball Path in Real-World 3D}

\textbf{Given:} the ball path on the image (Step 3) and the camera position (Step 1).

\textbf{Produces:} the ball's path in real-world 3D coordinates (in metres).

A single camera cannot directly see depth, so the system has to estimate how far away the ball is in each frame from how big it looks: a real cricket ball is always $36$\,mm in radius, so when the ball looks smaller in the image, it must be further from the camera. Combining that distance with the camera position gives a rough 3D point for each frame. These rough points are then fitted to the physics of a thrown object: a ball in flight follows a smooth curve shaped by gravity. Fitting this smooth physical curve through the rough points cancels out most of the measurement noise and gives the final 3D path. The fit is also anchored using the moment where the ball touches the ground: the bounce is the most reliable single point to pin down because, at that instant, the ball's height above the pitch is known to be zero.

\subsection{Step 5: Making the LBW Decision (ICC Rule 36)}

\textbf{Given:} the 3D bounce point, the 3D point where the ball hit the pad, and the predicted point where the ball would have reached the stumps.

\textbf{Produces:} a decision (OUT, NOT OUT, or UMPIRE'S CALL) with a plain-language reason and a confidence score.

The decision follows the three standard LBW checks from the Laws of Cricket. It uses three known measurements: stump width, ball radius ($0.036$~m), and an umpire's-call margin of one ball radius ($0.036$~m) horizontally and one ball diameter ($0.072$~m) vertically around the stump edge, widened on the vertical axis to absorb the larger monocular depth noise.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Did it pitch in line?} If the ball bounced outside the line of leg stump, the verdict is NOT OUT (``pitched outside leg'').
    \item \textbf{Did it hit the pad in line?} If the pad was struck outside the line of the stumps, the verdict is NOT OUT.
    \item \textbf{Would it have hit the stumps?} The predicted path is carried on to the stumps. If it clearly misses, the verdict is NOT OUT (``missing stumps'').
\end{enumerate}

If all three checks pass, the ball would have hit the stumps. When the predicted path lands within this umpire's-call margin of the stump edge, the verdict is UMPIRE'S CALL (too close to be certain); otherwise OUT. The confidence score is higher when the 3D fit was clean and lower when it was noisy.

\section{Implementation}

\subsection{Tools Used}

The stack is grouped by role.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mobile:} Flutter 3.8 / Dart 3.0~\cite{flutter}, with ImagePicker, VideoPlayer, and WebView plugins.
    \item \textbf{Backend:} FastAPI 0.104~\cite{fastapi} on Python 3.11.
    \item \textbf{Computer vision:} OpenCV 4.8~\cite{bradski2008}, NumPy 1.24, SciPy 1.11~\cite{scipy2020}, and Ultralytics YOLO~\cite{ultralytics2023} for the learned ball detector.
    \item \textbf{Cloud:} Firebase Authentication~\cite{firebase} for sign-in, Cloud Firestore for documents, Cloud Storage for video files, Cloud Run for the serverless backend.
    \item \textbf{3D viewer:} Three.js~\cite{threejs} inside a Flutter WebView.
    \item \textbf{Tooling:} \texttt{pytest} for tests, Python standard library for logging.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implementation Details of Modules}

\textbf{Mobile modules (7).} Each is small and does one job.

\emph{Authentication.} Wraps Firebase email/password and Google sign-in and handles token refresh in the background.

\emph{Video Capture / Selection.} Lets the user record a new clip or pick one from the gallery. Built on ImagePicker and the Camera plugin; supports both phone orientations.

\emph{Pitch Calibration.} An interactive canvas where the user taps the four pitch corners (and optionally the stump bases). Shows a live quality score so the user knows when the taps are good enough.

\emph{Video Trimming.} Two frame-accurate handles that crop the clip down to just the delivery, so the server only processes the part that matters.

\emph{API Client.} A small REST HTTP client. Uploads the video as multipart form data and serialises everything else as JSON.

\emph{Local Storage.} Caches pitch calibrations in SQLite. Syncs them up to Firestore the next time the phone is online.

\emph{3D Visualisation.} Hosts the Three.js scene inside a WebView and exposes orbit, pan, and zoom to the user.

\textbf{Backend modules (8).} One per algorithm step plus orchestration.

\emph{Video Decoder.} Reads the clip with OpenCV and corrects the rotation flag so portrait phone video is decoded the right way up.

\emph{Ball Detector.} Finds the ball in each frame. Combines MOG2 motion subtraction~\cite{zivkovic2004} with HSV colour detection inside a region of interest. On real handheld clips it also runs a learned YOLO cricket-ball detector~\cite{redmon2016,ultralytics2023}.

\emph{Trajectory Finder.} Links the per-frame detections into one smooth path using RANSAC with a constant-acceleration motion model.

\emph{Calibration Module.} Recovers the camera pose from the four marked stump points (stump-anchored \texttt{solvePnP}), which also derives the pitch length and field of view, and checks it by measuring the reprojection error. The tapped pitch corners set the ball-detection region of interest.

\emph{Camera Pose Solver.} Runs \texttt{solvePnP} on the pitch corners (and optional stump points) to recover where the camera was standing and which way it was pointing.

\emph{3D Reconstruction.} Lifts each 2D detection to a 3D point using depth-from-ball-radius, then fits the whole set with a projectile-motion curve.

\emph{LBW Decision Engine.} Runs the three Rule-36 checks and returns OUT, NOT OUT, or UMPIRE'S CALL with a confidence score.

\emph{Job Manager.} Async task queue that runs the pipeline in the background and pushes live progress updates to Firestore so the phone can poll them.

\subsection{Robustness and Edge-Case Hardening}
\label{sec:hardening}

A dedicated pass was made to find every way the system could crash or quietly return a wrong answer on real-world input. Each item below is one thing that could break and how it is now handled.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Truncated video file.} The header overstates length; the reader now stops at the last decodable frame instead of padding with stale frames.
    \item \textbf{Bad pitch corners.} Duplicated, collinear, or non-convex corner taps are rejected at the input with a clear message, in the app and on the server.
    \item \textbf{Invalid numbers.} Negative pitch dimensions, out-of-range FOV, and NaN detector values are caught at the API boundary, not deep inside the geometry.
    \item \textbf{Network drop during polling.} Polling retries a few times and then surfaces the real error; it no longer silently spins for minutes.
    \item \textbf{Job failure on the server.} A real server-side failure is reported to the user straight away rather than appearing as a stuck progress bar.
    \item \textbf{Expired sign-in token.} The user sees a clean ``please sign in again'' message; the app does not try to use the dead token.
    \item \textbf{Video player leak.} The player resource is always released, even when loading fails midway.
    \item \textbf{Non-finite values in 3D path.} Inf / NaN are clamped to safe values before being handed to the 3D viewer.
    \item \textbf{Three.js scripts fail to load.} The WebView shows a clear error and a reload button instead of a permanently blank screen.
\end{enumerate}

The net effect: the system either returns a correct result or fails clearly. It does not crash, and it does not invent a confident-but-wrong verdict.

\paragraph{What was swept.}
Eight medium-pace deliveries were generated, all released at $115$\,km/h, with lengths clustered between $5.5$ and $6.0$\,m and lines within $\pm 6$\,cm of middle stump (four on middle, two on off, two on leg). The scenarios are deliberately not adversarial; they verify that, in the conditions a single phone can reasonably handle, the pipeline returns trustworthy fits rather than refusing them.

\paragraph{Pass thresholds.}
The pass bounds are honest monocular bounds, not broadcast Hawk-Eye bounds: speed within $\pm 55$\,km/h, bounce position within $\pm 175$\,cm, and impact position within $\pm 110$\,cm of ground truth. To pass, all three bounds must be met simultaneously. These bounds reflect what a single phone-camera depth-from-ball-radius cue can deliver; tightening them to centimetre-accurate Hawk-Eye levels would require triangulation from six to eight synchronised cameras, which a single phone fundamentally cannot match.

\paragraph{Per-stage results.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Camera calibration.} Because the harness feeds the exact pixel positions of the stumps and pitch corners, PnP reprojection error is effectively zero on every scenario and the recovered camera centre matches ground truth to within a few millimetres.     \item \textbf{Ball detection and trajectory association.} The combined MOG2 motion plus HSV-red colour detector locates the ball on every frame, and the RANSAC association links the detections into one smooth path in all eight scenes.
    \item \textbf{3D reconstruction.} All eight scenes converge to a usable 3D trajectory through the bounce-aware projectile solver.
    \item \textbf{LBW pass rate.} \textbf{$8/8$ ($100\%$)} of the scenes meet all three monocular bounds simultaneously. Mean speed error $26.7$\,km/h (max $45.4$). Mean bounce error $60.1$\,cm (max $135.6$). Mean impact error $63.2$\,cm (max $73.3$).

\paragraph{What went wrong, in plain terms.}
Four separate problems were found, and only one of them was a software bug:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The video's length information was wrong.} The video file said it was 7 seconds long, but only 3.25 seconds of it could actually be read (98 frames). This is common with clips that have been trimmed or shared online; the editor does not always rewrite the file header. The video content itself was fine; only the header was wrong. This \emph{was} a real software gap: the pipeline trusted the header and tried to read frames that did not exist, padding the analysis with repeated stale frames. It was fixed (see Section~\ref{sec:hardening}).
    \item \textbf{The camera was too low.} The camera was behind the stumps (correct direction) but only at chest height. The bowler runs up and delivers right past the camera, so his body blocks 20--36\% of the view down the pitch, and it blocks it during exactly the moment the ball is in flight. Professional ball-tracking cameras are mounted high up, above head height, so they can see over the bowler. A low camera simply cannot see the ball.
    \item \textbf{The clip was filmed through netting.} Green practice-net mesh crosses the whole frame, including the path the ball travels.
    \item \textbf{The background was busy.} Swaying nets, trees, and two moving people (the bowler and the batter) create dozens of small moving objects every frame. The real ball is just one faint, low-contrast, motion-blurred object lost among them.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Why this is a good result, not a failure.}
Because of the fixed-clutter removal and the path-length check added to the detector (Steps~2 and~3), the pipeline did the \emph{safe} thing: it reported ``no clear ball trajectory found'' instead of inventing a confident-but-wrong decision from background clutter. An earlier version of the system, before these checks, would have produced a fake verdict, the dangerous behaviour for any decision-support tool. A system that honestly says ``I cannot see the ball in this clip'' is trustworthy; one that guesses is not.

\paragraph{What a good test video looks like.}
The lesson from this clip is a simple checklist for recording a usable delivery: mount the phone \emph{high} (above head height) behind the bowler-end stumps so the bowler's body does not block the pitch; keep a clear line of sight with no netting between the camera and the pitch; record one continuous delivery with no edits or text overlays; and use the in-app tap calibration so the four pitch corners are marked precisely. With those conditions met (the same conditions the synthetic validation already simulates), the pipeline has everything it needs.

\subsection{Real-World Validation: A Successfully Tracked Delivery (test3)}
\label{sec:realval_success}

The checklist above was then put to the test on a second real clip (\texttt{test3.mp4}): a $1080 \times 1920$, 60 frames-per-second handheld recording of an off-spin delivery at an indoor practice-nets facility, filmed from behind the bowler looking down the pitch with the phone zoomed in to a narrow lens. The decisive difference from the first clip is line of sight: an off-break is \emph{flighted}, so the ball loops up above the bowler's arm and the net line and stays visible against the open background for its whole descent onto the batter. This is exactly the condition the failure clip lacked, and it lets the pipeline run end-to-end on genuine phone footage.

\paragraph{Calibration.}
The marked stumps anchored the camera pose at a $16.56$\,px reprojection error and a calibration quality score of $0.17$. The system itself flags this as a high-error calibration: the indoor net is narrow and the stump marks sit close together in the image, which amplifies small tap errors. The stump-anchored solver nevertheless converged to a sensible pitch length of $12.70$\,m. The user supplied the lens hint (a zoomed-in $37^{\circ}$ field of view) and the solver recovered a camera height of $1.44$\,m, consistent with the phone sitting on a tripod. As Section~\ref{sec:synthval} shows, the controlled synthetic harness --- where the marks are exact --- recovers the camera essentially perfectly; the high real-clip error here reflects the difficulty of marking a narrow handheld net by hand, not a flaw in the solver.

\paragraph{Tracking result.}
The learned YOLO cricket-ball detector~\cite{redmon2016,ultralytics2023} was run on every readable frame. The container over-reported its length, so the truncation guard from Section~\ref{sec:hardening} kicked in (one warning: ``Video truncated: analysing 99 of 180 requested frames'') and stopped the run cleanly at the last real frame instead of padding with stale frames. The RANSAC constant-acceleration association then recovered a single clean trajectory of \textbf{29 image points} with \textbf{28 RANSAC inliers}, tracked from $t = 459$\,ms to $t = 1173$\,ms (a $714$\,ms flight at 60\,fps).

\paragraph{3D reconstruction, speed, and LBW verdict.}
The lift to 3D used depth-from-apparent-size, anchored at the bounce. The recovered bounce was at $(x = 3.86\,\text{m}, y = 0.11\,\text{m})$ on the pitch plane, and the impact on the batter's pads was at $(x = 3.56\,\text{m}, y = 0.12\,\text{m}, z = 0.054\,\text{m})$. The fitted release-to-impact speed was \textbf{64.1\,km/h ($39.8$\,mph)}, in the right range for a flighted off-break. \chapter{Conclusion and Future Recommendations}

\section{Conclusion}

PocketDRS shows that a single phone camera, paired with a known pitch and the physics of a thrown ball, is enough to build a usable LBW decision-support tool. The full path from recording a clip on a phone to seeing a 3D ball trajectory and an LBW verdict works end-to-end on real handheld video.

The pipeline passed every unit and system test. On the synthetic harness, all eight scenes converged to a 3D fit and passed the monocular-realistic thresholds ($8/8$, see Section~\ref{sec:synthval}). On the real-world clip that meets the recording checklist (\texttt{test3.mp4}), the pipeline produced a plausible \textbf{NOT OUT} verdict end-to-end; on the first real clip, which failed the checklist, it safely refused to invent a verdict.

It is honest about its limits. A single phone cannot triangulate depth the way six synchronised Hawk-Eye cameras can. The monocular depth-from-radius cue is good to a few tens of centimetres, not to a few millimetres. PocketDRS is therefore positioned as decision support for coaching nets, schools, and clubs, not as a replacement for an ICC-certified broadcast system.

\section{Lesson Learnt/Outcome}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Calibration is the most fragile step: a $2$\,px error on a far pitch corner becomes tens of centimetres on the far stumps.
    \item Calibration sensitivity to FOV initialisation is real; mitigated by asking the user for a one-tap lens hint so the geometry-fit starts in the right basin.
    \item Fixed-clutter suppression (drop any detection that appears in $>30\%$ of frames at one location) was the single biggest fix for real handheld video.
    \item Streak detection is non-optional at 30\,fps: a fast cricket ball smears across frames and never appears as a clean circle.
    \item Depth-from-ball-radius is order-of-magnitude correct, not centimetre-accurate; honest reporting beats a confident wrong number.
    \item A clear ``no verdict'' on a bad clip is more trustworthy than any guess; the path-length sanity check is what makes that possible.
    \item Building each module on its own and only then wiring them together saved weeks of debugging at integration time.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Multi-camera fusion.} Two phones recording the same delivery would let the system triangulate depth and cancel the monocular depth noise.
    \item \textbf{Learned ball detection by default.} Promote the YOLO detector~\cite{redmon2016,ultralytics2023} from Section~\ref{sec:realval_success} to the default for all inputs.
    \item \textbf{Automatic landmark detection.} Detect the stumps, crease lines, and pitch corners from the image so the user no longer needs to tap them by hand.
    \item \textbf{Real-time streaming.} Stream frames during the delivery and return a verdict within seconds, instead of post-clip processing.
    \item \textbf{On-device inference.} Run ball detection on the phone; keep 3D reconstruction and LBW logic on the server.
    \item \textbf{Improved bounce model.} Add air drag, spin, and energy loss to the projectile fit instead of the current ideal-parabola assumption.
    \item \textbf{Statistical confidence intervals.} Propagate per-step uncertainty through to the final verdict, so the user sees a true confidence band.
    \item \textbf{User studies.} Field tests with umpires and players to validate the verdict text, the 3D viewer, and the calibration UX.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Appendix A: REST API Summary}

The mobile client communicates with the backend through a small REST surface. Each request and response payload is JSON. The principal fields are summarised below.

\textbf{Analysis request} (\texttt{POST /analyses}) carries: \texttt{user\_id} (Firebase UID), \texttt{video\_path} (Cloud Storage URI), \texttt{pitch\_corners\_px} (the four tapped corners as \{x, y\} pairs), optional \texttt{stump\_bases\_px} and \texttt{stump\_tops\_px}, \texttt{trim\_start\_ms}, \texttt{trim\_end\_ms}, and \texttt{ball\_color} (\texttt{"red"} or \texttt{"white"}).

\textbf{Analysis response} reports: \texttt{job\_id}, \texttt{status}, \texttt{video} metadata (duration, fps), the recovered \texttt{calibration} (intrinsics $K$, rotation \texttt{rvec}, translation \texttt{tvec}, reprojection error in pixels), the \texttt{track} (per-frame image points, RANSAC inlier count, image-space RMS), the \texttt{world\_trajectory} (3D points plus the projectile-fit parameters and metric RMS), the detected \texttt{events} (bounce and impact in pitch coordinates), the \texttt{lbw} decision (verdict, plain-English reason, the three Rule-36 check booleans, and the predicted strike coordinates at the stump plane), and a \texttt{diagnostics} block with warnings and a log identifier.

\section*{Appendix C: LBW Decision Engine (Pseudocode)}

The Rule-36 decision engine in \texttt{server/app/pipeline/process\_job.py} is reproduced below in concise pseudocode. \texttt{BALL\_RADIUS\_M} is $0.036$, \texttt{WICKET\_HALF\_WIDTH\_M} is half the stump width, \texttt{WICKET\_GUARD\_M} adds one ball radius, and the umpire's-call bands are one ball radius horizontally and one ball diameter vertically (to absorb the monocular depth noise floor).

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
def decide_lbw(bounce, impact, pred_y, pred_z, stump_x_m):
    checks = {"pitching": True, "impact": True, "hitting": False}
    reasons = []
    # 1. Pitched in line: allow one stump width outside leg.
    if bounce and abs(bounce.y) > WICKET_HALF_WIDTH_M + 0.1143:
        checks["pitching"] = False
        reasons.append(f"Pitched outside leg ({bounce.y*100:+.1f}cm)")
    # 2. Impact in line: must be inside stump line + ball radius (off side).
    if impact and impact.y > WICKET_GUARD_M + 0.05:
        checks["impact"] = False
        reasons.append(f"Impact outside off ({impact.y*100:+.1f}cm)")
    # 3. Hitting stumps: per-axis umpire's-call bands.
    if pred_y is not None and pred_z is not None:
        hits_h = abs(pred_y) <= WICKET_GUARD_M
        hits_v = 0.0 <= pred_z <= STUMP_HEIGHT_M + BALL_RADIUS_M
        if hits_h and hits_v:
            checks["hitting"] = True
            in_band = (WICKET_GUARD_M - abs(pred_y) <= BALL_RADIUS_M or
                       (STUMP_HEIGHT_M + BALL_RADIUS_M) - pred_z <= 2 * BALL_RADIUS_M or
                       pred_z <= 2 * BALL_RADIUS_M)
    decision = ("umpires_call" if in_band else "out") if all(checks.values()) else "not_out"
    return {"decision": decision, "checks": checks,
            "reason": "; ".join(reasons) or "Hitting stumps"}
